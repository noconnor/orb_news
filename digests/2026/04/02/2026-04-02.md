> **Historical Parallel: The Tanker War** (1984-1988)
>
> The Tanker War was a sustained phase of the broader Iran-Iraq War in which both belligerents systematically attacked commercial shipping in the Persian Gulf to strangle each other's oil revenues and coerce third parties. Iraq opened the campaign in March 1984 by striking Iran's main export terminal at Kharg Island; Iran retaliated by targeting tankers servicing Gulf states that backed Iraq. Over the conflict, Iraq carried out 283 vessel attacks and Iran 168, collectively damaging more than 30 million tons of cargo and killing over 100 sailors. Shipping insurance premiums surged, global oil markets were repeatedly rattled, and the Strait of Hormuz hovered as a constant flashpoint. The United States intervened from 1986 onward, reflagging Kuwaiti tankers as American vessels and mounting Operation Earnest Will — the largest naval convoy operation since World War II. Direct US-Iranian naval clashes followed, culminating in Operation Praying Mantis in April 1988, in which the US Navy destroyed a significant portion of Iran's operational naval capacity in a single day. Iran's acceptance of a UN ceasefire in August 1988 brought the broader war to a close without permanent border changes.
>
> The current situation mirrors the Tanker War in its core dynamic: Iran using the Persian Gulf as a theatre of asymmetric pressure on a stronger adversary, with the Strait of Hormuz serving as leverage over global energy markets. The critical difference is the scale of direct US involvement — in 1984-1988, Washington intervened defensively to protect neutral shipping; the current conflict involves direct US-Israeli offensive operations against Iran itself, representing a materially higher level of escalation. The historical record suggests that sustained military pressure eventually degraded Iran's willingness to absorb costs, but the process took years and required significant Western naval commitment before the strategic calculus shifted.

### 2. NASA's Artemis II launches on historic crewed journey around the moon

<table><tr><td>

After years of delays and technical setbacks, NASA's Artemis II mission successfully launched from Florida on April 2, marking humanity's first crewed lunar voyage in over 50 years [[1]](https://www.npr.org/2026/04/02/nx-s1-5770599/artemis-ii-10-day-mission-preview). Four astronauts are now in Earth orbit beginning their 10-day mission that will take them around the moon without landing. The mission represents a crucial test of NASA's new Space Launch System rocket and Orion spacecraft ahead of planned lunar landings in subsequent missions [[2]](https://www.bbc.com/news/articles/c7vqre914pzo). Thousands gathered in Florida to witness the historic launch, with widespread excitement about humanity's return to lunar exploration after more than half a century [[1]](https://www.npr.org/2026/04/02/nx-s1-5770599/artemis-ii-10-day-mission-preview).

</td><td width="200"><img src="https://ichef.bbci.co.uk/ace/standard/240/cpsprodpb/fb11/live/03b36c00-2e8e-11f1-bf7c-65962cff9746.png" width="200"></td></tr></table>

The Artemis II flight will gather critical data on spacecraft performance, crew health, and deep-space navigation for upcoming landing missions and long-term lunar operations [[1]](https://www.npr.org/2026/04/02/nx-s1-5770599/artemis-ii-10-day-mission-preview). This launch coincides with SpaceX advancing toward a projected $1 trillion IPO that could make Elon Musk the world's first trillionaire, underscoring the extraordinary pace of transformation in both government and private space sectors [[2]](https://www.bbc.com/news/articles/c7vqre914pzo). The dual developments signal a new era in human space exploration, with commercial and governmental missions increasingly pushing boundaries together.

> **Historical Parallel: Apollo 8** (1968)
>
> Apollo 8 launched on December 21, 1968, as the first crewed mission to travel beyond Earth orbit and reach the Moon — not to land, but to orbit it and return, validating the technology and procedures needed for a landing. The mission was conceived under significant pressure: Soviet advances, particularly the unmanned Zond 5 circumlunar flight in September 1968, threatened to claim the symbolic prize of first humans near the Moon, prompting NASA to dramatically accelerate its timeline. Frank Borman, Jim Lovell, and William Anders became the first humans to pass through the Van Allen radiation belts, achieve lunar orbit, and witness an Earthrise with their own eyes. The crew completed ten lunar orbits over twenty hours, conducted reconnaissance of the proposed Apollo 11 landing site, and validated trans-lunar injection and orbital insertion techniques essential for subsequent missions. Their Christmas Eve television broadcast, in which they read from the Book of Genesis, was watched by an estimated 25 percent of the world's population, and their photograph of the Earth rising over the lunar horizon became one of the most reproduced images in history. Time magazine named the crew Men of the Year for 1968, a year scarred by assassinations, war, and civil upheaval. Apollo 8's success led directly to Apollo 10, a full dress rehearsal, and then to Apollo 11's landing in July 1969 — just seven months after Apollo 8 had first proven the path was navigable.
>
> Artemis II occupies structurally the same position in NASA's current lunar programme that Apollo 8 occupied in the original Apollo sequence: a crewed flyby mission whose primary purpose is to validate hardware, demonstrate deep-space navigation, and build the confidence required before a landing attempt. The broader contexts differ — Apollo 8 was driven by acute Cold War competition with a credible rival, while Artemis operates in a more diffuse competitive environment with China's lunar programme as a longer-horizon concern — but the logic of a pathfinder mission reducing uncertainty before commitment to a landing is identical. The historical record from Apollo 8 is instructive: the gap between a successful crewed flyby and a first landing was remarkably short once the foundational mission succeeded, suggesting that Artemis II's outcome will be a decisive factor in compressing or extending the timeline to an actual lunar surface return.
>
