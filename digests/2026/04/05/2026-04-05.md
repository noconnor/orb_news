### US rescues airman shot down over Iran as conflict enters its 37th day

<!-- audio:top-story-1 -->

The multi-front nature of the strikes signals a deliberate Iranian strategy to draw in Gulf state proxies and threaten critical infrastructure well beyond the direct US-Iran bilateral engagement, with consequences rippling through global energy markets and regional stability <sup>[5](https://www.aljazeera.com/video/newsfeed/2026/4/5/yemens-houthis-claim-cluster-missile-attack-on-israels-ben-gurion-airport?traffic_source=rss)</sup>.

### Ukraine's €90 billion EU lifeline blocked by Hungary ahead of Orbán election

<!-- audio:top-story-3 -->
Ukrainian President Volodymyr Zelenskyy expressed open frustration at EU foreign ministers' talks over Hungary's continued blockade of a €90 billion EU loan to Kyiv, a critical financial lifeline that has become entangled in the electoral calculations of Viktor Orbán ahead of Hungary's April 12 parliamentary vote <sup>[1](https://www.theguardian.com/world/ukraine)</sup>. With US financial support for Ukraine reduced under the Trump administration, the EU loan represents one of the principal mechanisms through which Europe has sought to sustain Ukraine's economic and military capacity — making Orbán's veto not merely a bilateral irritant but a structural threat to European unity on Ukraine support <sup>[2](http://www.euronews.com/my-europe)</sup>. Orbán, trailing in polls for the first time in over a decade, is using anti-EU and anti-Ukraine rhetoric to energise his nationalist base, a strategy that has collided directly with the bloc's collective interest in maintaining Kyiv's solvency.

The episode is complicated further by the broader context of Ukraine's mounting pressures. Russia launched 286 long-range drones overnight in one of its most intense aerial campaigns, with Ukrainian forces intercepting 260 — a figure that underscores both the resilience of Ukraine's air defences and the unsustainable rate at which interception resources are being consumed <sup>[3](https://www.france24.com/en/europe/20260404-russian-drone-strike-on-ukraine-market-kills-five-wounds-19)</sup>. A Russian drone strike on Nikopol's covered market killed five civilians and wounded thirty others, the kind of deliberate targeting of civilian gathering points that has characterised Russia's aerial terror campaign throughout the war <sup>[4](https://www.france24.com/en/several-people-killed-in-ukraine-after-a-russian-drone-strike-on-a-market)</sup>. Ukrainian forces simultaneously struck Russian energy infrastructure, hitting the Primorsk fuel reservoir and the NORSI refinery near Nizhny Novgorod in a continuing campaign to degrade Russia's ability to fund and supply its military operations <sup>[5](https://www.aljazeera.com/news/2026/4/5/drone-attacks-hit-russian-oil-infrastructure-leak-and-fires-reported?traffic_source=rss)</sup>.

## Ireland

<!-- audio:ireland -->
The most striking domestic story of the Easter weekend concerns not politics or weather but a cold case that has haunted Irish public memory for over four decades. Gardaí have submitted extensive investigation files on the 1984 Kerry Babies case to the Director of Public Prosecutions, representing the most significant procedural development in the case since the original controversy <sup>[1](https://www.thejournal.ie/baby-john-kerry-babies-case-7004027-Apr2026/)</sup>. The case centres on an infant, named "Baby John" by local authorities, whose body was discovered on White Strand beach in Caherciveen on April 14, 1984, bearing 28 stab wounds — a case that became notorious not only for its unresolved circumstances but for the manner in which Garda investigators subsequently handled suspects, giving rise to one of Ireland's most controversial criminal justice episodes. The submission to the DPP after 42 years represents a renewed institutional commitment to achieving some form of closure, though whether prosecution will follow remains uncertain <sup>[2](https://www.irishexaminer.com/news/munster/arid-41822255.html)</sup>.

Storm Dave defined the practical experience of Easter weekend for much of the country, bringing severe winds that caused widespread power outages across thousands of homes and businesses, cancelled flights, and Status Orange weather warnings across multiple counties <sup>[3](https://www.irishtimes.com/environment/2026/04/05/storm-dave-ireland-weather-updates/)</sup>. Infrastructure repair teams worked through the weekend to restore electricity to affected areas, with the storm's impact on transport compounding the disruption for families travelling during one of the busiest holiday periods of the year <sup>[4](https://www.thejournal.ie/weather-forecast-easter-sunday-2-7004054-Apr2026/)</sup>. The storm arrived alongside a sharp road safety story: Gardaí detected more than 2,100 speeding violations in the first 72 hours of an intensive Easter bank holiday enforcement operation, a figure that sits uneasily alongside two fatal traffic accidents in the Dublin area — an 80-year-old woman struck by a bus in Blackrock, and a man in his 30s fatally injured in a separate incident <sup>[5](https://www.rte.ie/news/ireland/2026/0405/1566802-garda-road-safety/)</sup> <sup>[6](https://www.thejournal.ie/woman-dies-hit-by-bus-blackrock-7004043-Apr2026/)</sup>.

On the housing front, two data points reinforced the scale of Ireland's affordability crisis. An analysis of European rental markets ranked Dublin among the continent's most expensive, surpassed only by a handful of major financial centres, with the findings underlining the structural nature of a problem that has resisted successive policy interventions <sup>[7](https://www.rte.ie/news/business/2026/0405/1566492-rents-europe/)</sup>. Separately, TheJournal.ie's FactCheck column examined a Dáil claim by Labour TD Conor Sheehan that Irish evictions are now at their highest rate since the Great Famine, a striking assertion that situates Ireland's contemporary housing pressures within a very long historical arc <sup>[8](https://www.thejournal.ie/factcheck-evictions-highest-since-irish-famine-7001541-Apr2026/)</sup>.

The weekend also produced a notable political data point and a quieter social one. A Sunday Independent/Ireland Thinks poll found Fianna Fáil leader Micheál Martin and Social Democrats leader Holly Cairns tied at 39% approval each, a result that signals competitive political dynamics ahead of future electoral cycles and reflects the fragmented nature of post-coalition public sentiment <sup>[9](https://www.thejournal.ie/micheal-martin-holly-cairns-support-poll-7004076-Apr2026/)</sup>. Meanwhile, a debate is emerging among parents and educators about the extent of classroom technology use, with several countries having already moved away from digital-only learning — Irish stakeholders are now weighing whether tablet and laptop dependence may be harming student outcomes and wellbeing in ways that warrant a recalibration of educational technology strategy <sup>[10](https://www.thejournal.ie/digital-devices-laptops-in-school-7002020-Apr2026/)</sup>.

## Afternoon Update _(16:45 IST)_

The afternoon brought a significant new development in European energy security: Serbian President Aleksandar Vučić announced that explosives had been discovered near a pipeline carrying Russian gas to Serbia and Hungary, raising immediate concerns about the sabotage of a strategic supply artery in southeastern Europe <sup>[1](http://www.euronews.com/my-europe/2026/04/05/serbian-president-explosives-found-near-gas-pipeline-to-hungary)</sup>. The discovery comes at a moment when European energy infrastructure is already under intense scrutiny following the Nord Stream pipeline sabotage and repeated discussions about the continent's dependence on Russian energy supply routes. The pipeline in question serves as a critical link connecting Russian gas exports to two EU and EU-adjacent states, and its disruption — had the explosives detonated — would have compounded the fuel price pressures already tightening across the continent. Serbian authorities have not yet attributed responsibility publicly, but the incident is likely to accelerate calls for enhanced physical security across European pipeline networks and may prompt fresh EU-level discussions about infrastructure protection protocols.

The afternoon's European data releases added texture to the labour market picture across the continent. A Randstad workforce study confirmed Portugal ranks among the EU's most demanding working environments, with employees averaging close to 50 hours per week — placing the country fourth among member states for extended working hours <sup>[2](http://www.euronews.com/business/2026/04/05/portugal-among-eu-countries-with-the-most-people-working-close-to-50-hours-a-week)</sup>. The finding reflects broader structural pressures in southern European labour markets, where weaker collective bargaining and greater economic precarity push workers toward longer hours despite EU working-time regulations intended to cap the working week. The contrast with northern European states — where shorter working weeks are both legally enforced and culturally embedded — underlines persistent regulatory divergence within the single market that has direct implications for worker health and productivity.

On a quieter but ecologically significant note, health authorities across Europe drew attention to the changing character of pollen seasons: warmer temperatures and shifted seasonal patterns have extended the allergy period for millions of Europeans, with pollen now present across more months of the year than was typical two decades ago <sup>[3](http://www.euronews.com/health/2026/04/05/pollen-is-here-to-stay-as-seasons-get-longer-heres-how-to-protect-yourself)</sup>. Public health guidance has shifted accordingly, with recommendations moving away from seasonal management toward year-round regimens for allergy sufferers. The trend is a ground-level illustration of how climate-driven ecological change is reshaping daily life for ordinary Europeans — mundane in scale but cumulative in public health cost.

Ireland marked an important moment of national reflection on Easter Sunday as the annual commemoration of the 1916 Easter Rising took place on O'Connell Street in Dublin, attended by President Michael D. Higgins and Taoiseach Micheál Martin <sup>[4](https://www.rte.ie/news/ireland/2026/0405/1566800-easter-rising-commemoration/)</sup>. This year's ceremony marked the 110th anniversary of the insurrection that set Ireland on the path to independence, and it unfolded against an Easter weekend already marked by Storm Dave's disruption, road safety concerns, and the sobering Kerry Babies development — a combination that gave the commemorations an unusually layered emotional register. The Rising's centennial was celebrated ten years ago with considerable ceremonial weight; the 110th anniversary is a quieter occasion but no less significant as a moment for the state to restate its foundational narrative at a time when Irish political culture is in flux.
