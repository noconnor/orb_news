>

Crude prices surged from around $17 per barrel in July 1990 to a peak of $46 per barrel by mid-October — a near-tripling in under three months. The shock contributed to the early 1990s recession in the United States and rattled economies worldwide. Unlike the 1973 embargo or the 1979 revolution-driven crisis, this shock was the product of direct military aggression against a major oil-producing state rather than a political decision to restrict supply. A US-led multinational coalition assembled under UN authority and expelled Iraqi forces from Kuwait by late February 1991, reassuring markets about future supply security.

The key structural difference is that in 1990 there was a clear external aggressor against whom a coalition could be mobilised, whereas today the belligerents include the United States itself, removing the coalition-resolution pathway. The historical record suggests that when conflict directly threatens Gulf producing capacity, markets price in the disruption rapidly and severely — but that perception of a credible security response can stabilise prices faster than the underlying physical damage would imply.

### 2. NASA's Artemis II lunar mission successfully leaves Earth orbit, heading for the Moon

NASA's Artemis II spacecraft executed a critical Trans-Lunar Injection burn on April 3, successfully leaving Earth's orbit and setting course for the Moon for the first time since the Apollo era <sup>[1](https://www.npr.org/2026/04/03/nx-s1-5771567/nasa-artemis-ii-tli-moon)</sup>. Four astronauts are now on a trajectory toward the far side of the Moon, with the Orion capsule having proven its propulsion systems and navigation capabilities during the crucial engine firing. Mission controllers confirmed the spacecraft is on track for its planned lunar orbit insertion and safe return by late April.

This achievement represents a major milestone in humanity's return to lunar exploration after more than five decades, building upon decades of international space cooperation <sup>[2](https://www.bbc.com/news/articles/c937g7nd5x4o)</sup>. The mission will test life support systems and validate crew procedures needed for future Artemis missions, establishing the technical foundation for sustainable human presence on the Moon. The success comes amid intensifying international competition in space exploration, with multiple nations pursuing ambitious lunar programs <sup>[3](https://www.aljazeera.com/video/newsfeed/2026/4/3/artemis-ii-mission-fires-up-engines-in-next-step-of-moon-mission)</sup>.

> **Historical Parallel: Apollo 10** (1969)
>
> Apollo 10 launched on May 18, 1969, as the final full dress rehearsal for the first crewed Moon landing. Commanders Thomas Stafford and Gene Cernan piloted the Lunar Module 'Snoopy' to within 14.4 kilometres of the lunar surface — closer than any humans had come — before returning to rendezvous with John Young in the Command Module 'Charlie Brown'. The mission deliberately tested every phase of a landing mission except the final descent and touchdown itself, validating lunar orbit operations, landing site reconnaissance at the Sea of Tranquility, and the critical rendezvous procedures. NASA intentionally underfuelled the ascent stage so that a landing attempt was physically impossible, ensuring the historic first landing would not occur prematurely on a test flight. The mission encountered a near-catastrophic equipment problem when an inadvertent switch setting caused the Lunar Module to tumble violently during ascent stage separation; Stafford regained manual control within seconds. Apollo 10's data allowed NASA flight readiness teams to clear Apollo 11 for the landing just two months later, in July 1969.
>
> Artemis II occupies a structurally similar role to Apollo 10 — a crewed deep-space mission designed to validate hardware, navigation, and crew procedures one step short of a surface landing. The historical record suggests that when the dress rehearsal succeeds cleanly, the window to the landing mission can be remarkably short; Apollo 10 cleared the way for Apollo 11 in under two months. The critical difference is that Artemis II does not carry a Lunar Module, making it closer in profile to Apollo 8 than Apollo 10, but either precedent points toward the same implication: successful completion of this mission would meaningfully advance the timeline to Artemis III's landing attempt.
>

> **Historical Parallel: The 8888 Uprising and SLORC Rule** (1988-1990)
>
> In August 1988, a decade of economic mismanagement under General Ne Win's one-party rule culminated in nationwide mass protests across Burma, with demonstrations in Rangoon drawing crowds of over half a million. Two successive currency demonetisations had wiped out citizens' savings without compensation, and students, monks, workers, and ordinary citizens united in demanding multi-party democracy. The military regime, having shed Ne Win and replaced him with a civilian figure, initially appeared to waver — but on September 18, 1988 hardline General Saw Maung orchestrated a coup and established the State Law and Order Restoration Council (SLORC). Troops fired indiscriminately on protesters across cities; estimates of the death toll range from 3,000 to 10,000. SLORC imposed martial law and cancelled all promises of democratic reform, exercising tighter controls than the Ne Win era had. When the military permitted elections in May 1990, Aung San Suu Kyi's National League for Democracy won an overwhelming victory — which the junta then simply voided, placing Suu Kyi under house arrest. The military maintained unchallenged authoritarian rule for more than two more decades, loosening its grip only gradually after 2011 before reasserting full control through the 2021 coup.
>
> The 8888 suppression established a pattern now repeating at a new stage: Myanmar's military responds to challenges to its authority not by accommodating opposition but by institutionalising its grip, using a veneer of formal governance structures to normalise what is fundamentally coercive control. Min Aung Hlaing's election as president by a pro-military parliament mirrors SLORC's 1990 election manipulation — the formal machinery of a state is being constructed around military power rather than replacing it. The historical record of post-1988 Burma suggests that international condemnation and economic isolation have had limited effect on the trajectory of military consolidation once the initial moment of mass uprising has been suppressed.

### New historical context for top stories

The afternoon synthesis has produced fresh historical parallels for two of today's top stories. On the Iran-US confrontation, analysts now draw comparison to the **Iran-Iraq War (1980–1988)** — a conflict whose notorious "Tanker War" sub-conflict mirrors current Hormuz disruptions with striking precision <sup>[1](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Iran%E2%80%93Iraq_War)</sup>. That war saw both sides systematically striking merchant shipping in the Persian Gulf to strangle each other's oil revenues, drawing in US naval forces and causing surges in insurance premiums on Gulf shipping that forced European energy planners to reckon with supply vulnerability — a dynamic playing out again today. The legal dimension is also familiar: the Iran-Iraq War saw international law invoked but largely unenforced, just as over 100 US legal experts are now alleging war crimes while formal accountability mechanisms remain slow to engage.

For Finland's intensified conscript training in response to Russian pressure, the parallel is the **Winter War (1939–1940)**, when Finland resisted a full-scale Soviet invasion for months, inflicting severe casualties before accepting territorial concessions under the Moscow Peace Treaty — while retaining its sovereignty <sup>[2](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Winter_War)</sup>. Finland's current defence posture — combining high military readiness with NATO membership — is a direct institutional descendant of that experience: the belief, encoded in Finnish defence culture, that a well-prepared conscript force can resist a vastly larger neighbour. Where the Winter War saw Finland standing largely alone, today's Finland benefits from collective defence guarantees that did not exist in 1939, a structural difference that may significantly alter the risk calculus on both sides.
