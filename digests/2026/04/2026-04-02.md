NASA's Artemis II mission lifted off from Kennedy Space Center on April 1, 2026, sending four astronauts on the first crewed journey to the vicinity of the Moon since the Apollo 17 mission in December 1972, marking the most significant milestone in human space exploration in more than half a century. The crew — NASA astronauts Reid Wiseman, Victor Glover, and Christina Koch, plus Canadian Space Agency astronaut Jeremy Hansen — break multiple historic records: Glover is the first person of colour, Koch the first woman, and Hansen the first non-US citizen ever to travel beyond low Earth orbit. The mission employs NASA's Space Launch System rocket and Orion spacecraft on a free-return lunar flyby lasting approximately ten days, with astronauts expected to reach lunar vicinity within three days and spend approximately one day observing the far side of the Moon. The crew will set a new record for the farthest distance ever travelled from Earth at approximately 252,000 miles, with splashdown planned in the Pacific Ocean off San Diego where the US Navy will recover the crew. Shortly after launch, the crew manually flew the Orion capsule as part of planned mission operations, demonstrating the vehicle's capabilities. Artemis II is the second flight of the SLS but the first to carry humans, representing a critical validation milestone before the planned Artemis III landing mission. The programme ultimately aims to return astronauts to the lunar surface and establish a sustained human presence near the Moon. The launch drew widespread international enthusiasm despite occurring against a backdrop of geopolitical turmoil, with observers describing it as an emotionally resonant and historic moment for human exploration. Covered extensively by NPR and BBC World, the mission was noted as a rare piece of broadly positive global news in an otherwise turbulent news cycle. The success of the mission is expected to reinvigorate public and political support for the broader Artemis programme and international lunar exploration cooperation.

## Historical Context

### Trump's Iran War Speech Draws Global Condemnation — Historical Parallel
> **1973 Oil Crisis (Arab Oil Embargo)** (1973–1974)

In October 1973, the Organization of Arab Petroleum Exporting Countries (OAPEC) imposed a total oil embargo on the United States, the Netherlands, and several other Western nations in response to US military support for Israel during the Yom Kippur War. Saudi Arabia led the effort, and Arab producers progressively cut output, reducing supply to roughly 25% of September 1973 levels by December. The price of oil rose from approximately $3 per barrel to nearly $12 per barrel by March 1974 — a roughly 300% increase — while Middle Eastern oil exports to the West fell by 60–70% at the peak of the disruption. Western nations scrambled to implement emergency rationing, speed limits, and daylight saving measures to conserve fuel, and the crisis triggered deep recessions across much of Europe and North America. The embargo demonstrated, for the first time at scale, that energy supply chains could be weaponised as geopolitical leverage during a Middle Eastern military conflict. King Faisal lifted the embargo in March 1974 after the United States offered more even-handed Middle East diplomacy and weapons sales to Saudi Arabia. The long-term legacy was a structural shift in Western energy policy toward domestic exploration, strategic petroleum reserves, and alternative energy research.

The current closure of the Strait of Hormuz — disrupting roughly one-quarter of global oil supply and driving EU gas prices up approximately 70% — mirrors the structural shock of the 1973 embargo: a Middle Eastern military conflict weaponising a critical energy chokepoint and forcing dependent economies into emergency posture. The key difference is that 1973 involved a deliberate, politically coordinated supply cut by producer states, whereas the 2026 disruption is a byproduct of active warfare closing a transit route. The historical record suggests that such shocks, while severe, have ultimately resolved once the underlying military or diplomatic conflict reached a threshold settlement, though they consistently left lasting imprints on energy policy and accelerated transitions away from single-point supply dependence.

### Russia Launches Largest Single-Day Drone Offensive — Historical Parallel
> **Tanker War (Iran-Iraq War)** (1981–1988)

The Tanker War was a sustained campaign of attrition strikes on commercial and military shipping in the Persian Gulf and Strait of Hormuz, conducted by both Iraq and Iran as part of the broader Iran-Iraq War. Iraq initiated attacks on Iranian oil tankers and the Kharg Island terminal from 1981, deliberately escalating in 1984 to provoke Iranian retaliation and, it hoped, international intervention. Iran responded with 168 of its own attacks on Gulf shipping; in total, 546 commercial vessels were damaged and approximately 430 civilian mariners were killed across the 1981–1988 period. The systematic targeting of economic infrastructure — oil terminals, tankers, and supply routes — was intended to exhaust the adversary's revenue and will to continue rather than achieve a decisive battlefield victory. The United States was eventually drawn in directly in 1987, reflagging Kuwaiti tankers under the American flag and launching Operation Earnest Will to escort them through the Gulf, following Iraq's accidental strike on the USS Stark. The conflict ended in August 1988 alongside the broader Iran-Iraq ceasefire, with neither side having achieved its core objectives and the region's shipping infrastructure severely damaged. The Tanker War is considered a foundational case study in the use of sustained, low-cost attrition strikes against economic targets as a substitute for decisive military engagement.

Russia's escalating drone campaign — reaching approximately 700 drones in a single day — echoes the Tanker War's logic of sustained attrition strikes against economic and civilian infrastructure as a means of grinding down an adversary's resilience when the front lines are stalemated. Both campaigns reveal how a belligerent unable or unwilling to achieve a swift conventional victory shifts toward mass, repetitive strikes on the opponent's energy grid and supply networks. The historical record from the Tanker War suggests that such campaigns, while destructive, tend to harden rather than break the targeted society's resolve, and that they persist until a diplomatic framework — often brokered under external pressure — creates conditions for de-escalation.

### NASA Artemis II Launches — Historical Parallel
> **Apollo 17 — Final Crewed Lunar Mission** (1972)

Apollo 17 launched on December 7, 1972, as the eleventh and final crewed mission of NASA's Apollo program, and the last time human beings travelled beyond low Earth orbit until Artemis II in 2026. Commander Gene Cernan and geologist Harrison Schmitt landed in the Taurus-Littrow valley while Ronald Evans orbited in the command module; the mission set records for the longest lunar surface stay (75 hours), greatest EVA distance from a spacecraft (7.6 km), and largest sample return (approximately 115 kg). The program had been progressively curtailed: Apollo 18, 19, and 20 were cancelled between 1970 and 1972 due to budget pressures and a shifting public and political consensus that the Moon race had been won and the cost could not be justified. Cernan's final words on the lunar surface — 'we leave as we came and, God willing, as we shall return' — captured a prevalent assumption at the time that a return within years was likely; in practice, no human left Earth orbit again for over half a century. The cancellation reflected a broader pattern in which transformative technological achievements, once stripped of their Cold War political urgency, struggle to sustain the institutional funding required for follow-on missions. Apollo 17's scientific yield — including the discovery of 3.5-billion-year-old volcanic orange soil — demonstrated how much remained scientifically unexplored, making the subsequent 53-year gap all the more striking in retrospect.

Artemis II is the direct historical successor to Apollo 17, and the parallel is structural rather than merely coincidental: both missions are defined by the political and institutional conditions that surround a crewed lunar program as much as by their technical achievements. Apollo 17 closed a program that was shuttered by budget pressures and the evaporation of Cold War urgency once the USSR had been beaten to the Moon; Artemis II reopens the same trajectory after a gap driven by decades of similarly contested funding cycles, competing priorities, and cancelled successor programs. The historical record suggests that the critical variable is not the engineering capability — which was proven in 1972 — but whether a sustained political and fiscal commitment can be maintained past the initial milestone, a challenge the Apollo program itself ultimately failed to meet.
