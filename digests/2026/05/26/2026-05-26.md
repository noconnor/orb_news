### US Strikes Iran as Nuclear Talks Hang in the Balance

<!-- audio:top-story-1 -->
American military forces struck Iranian targets in the Persian Gulf on 25–26 May, hitting missile launch sites and naval vessels in operations the Pentagon characterised as lawful acts of self-defence<sup>[1](https://www.aljazeera.com/video/newsfeed/2026/5/26/aje-onl-nf_reported-us-strikes-southern-iran-in-self-defence-250526)</sup>. The strikes landed even as Secretary of State Rubio indicated that a nuclear deal between Washington and Tehran could be finalised within days, underscoring the fragile coexistence of military pressure and diplomatic engagement that has characterised the standoff for months<sup>[2](https://www.npr.org/2026/05/25/nx-s1-5833690/u-s-iran-negotiations-updates)</sup>. Iranian officials moved swiftly to rebut suggestions that agreement was imminent, insisting significant gaps remain, while crude oil prices rose sharply on fears of further disruption to Gulf shipping lanes<sup>[3](https://www.bbc.com/news/articles/cvgzzn4y1n8o)</sup>.

The Strait of Hormuz — the narrow chokepoint through which roughly a fifth of the world's oil passes — remains the pivot around which the crisis turns. Any sustained interruption to tanker traffic would send energy prices surging across Europe and Asia and could imperil the fragile economic recoveries under way in several Western economies<sup>[4](https://news.google.com/rss/search?q=site:reuters.com&hl=en-US&gl=US&ceid=US:en)</sup>. The White House has not formally invoked the War Powers Resolution, leaving open legal questions about the extent of presidential authority to sustain unilateral military operations without congressional authorisation<sup>[5](https://news.google.com/rss/search?q=site:apnews.com&hl=en-US&gl=US&ceid=US:en)</sup>.

> **Historical Parallel: The Suez Crisis / Tanker War / Iran-Iraq War (1956–1988)**
> The current confrontation draws on several overlapping precedents of great-power competition over Gulf oil routes. During the 1956 Suez Crisis, Egypt's nationalisation of the canal triggered a military intervention by Britain, France and Israel — only for the United States to force their withdrawal, asserting that strategic waterways could not be controlled by force<sup>[a](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Suez_Crisis)</sup>. Two decades later the Iran-Iraq War's "Tanker War" phase saw both sides attack commercial shipping in the Gulf, eventually drawing in US naval escorts under Operation Earnest Will and culminating in direct American clashes with Iranian forces<sup>[b](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tanker_war)</sup>. The 1980 Operation Eagle Claw rescue mission — an earlier US military foray into Iranian territory that ended in catastrophe — and the subsequent War Powers debates in Congress<sup>[c](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Operation_Eagle_Claw)</sup><sup>[d](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/War_Powers_Resolution)</sup> all point to the recurring difficulty Washington faces in calibrating force against Tehran without triggering wider escalation. The current strikes follow that pattern precisely: limited enough to maintain deniability of full war, aggressive enough to signal resolve, yet leaving the diplomatic door deliberately ajar.

### Russia's Missile Blitz on Kyiv Reaches New Intensity

<!-- audio:top-story-2 -->
Russia launched one of its most intensive aerial campaigns against the Ukrainian capital over the 24–25 May weekend, firing hypersonic missiles and striking more than fifty targets including residential apartment blocks, schools and civilian infrastructure<sup>[1](https://www.france24.com/en/europe/20260525-russia-threatens-fresh-kyiv-strikes-warns-foreigners-to-leave)</sup>. The Russian foreign ministry followed the bombardment with an extraordinary warning — urging foreign diplomats and residents to evacuate Kyiv in anticipation of further, even heavier strikes<sup>[2](http://www.euronews.com/my-europe/2026/05/25/russia-urges-foreign-residents-and-diplomats-to-leave-kyiv-as-it-threatens-more-strikes)</sup>. Foreign Minister Lavrov publicly itemised prospective target sets, in what Western governments condemned as a deliberate act of psychological warfare designed to hollow out the Ukrainian capital's international presence and undermine its legitimacy as a functioning seat of government<sup>[3](https://www.bbc.com/news/articles/c1e22n55zn4o)</sup>.

Ukraine responded by intensifying its own long-range campaign against Russian energy and military infrastructure, striking oil refineries deep inside Russian territory<sup>[4](https://news.google.com/rss/search?q=site:reuters.com&hl=en-US&gl=US&ceid=US:en)</sup>. President Putin, meanwhile, announced debt-relief incentives for new military recruits, signalling that Russia intends to sustain the conflict indefinitely rather than negotiate from a position of weakness<sup>[5](https://www.bbc.com/news/articles/c1e22n55zn4o)</sup>. French and European Union leaders condemned the escalation in the strongest terms, while EU heads of state continue to debate accelerated military aid packages and the proposed interim "associate member" status for Ukraine championed by German Chancellor Merz.

> **Historical Parallel: The Blitz / Allied Strategic Bombing (1940–1945)**
> Russia's deliberate targeting of Ukrainian civilian infrastructure echoes the logic — and the moral catastrophe — of the German terror-bombing campaigns of the Second World War<sup>[a](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Strategic_bombing_during_World_War_II)</sup>. The Blitz against British cities between 1940 and 1941 was intended to break civilian morale and coerce capitulation; it achieved the opposite, hardening British resolve. The Allied oil campaign later in the war took the inverse approach, targeting industrial and energy infrastructure to degrade the enemy's capacity to fight<sup>[b](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Oil_campaign_of_World_War_II)</sup>. Ukraine now faces both dimensions simultaneously — its cities are bombed for psychological effect while its energy grid is systematically dismantled. The Korean War's grinding stalemate, in which neither side could achieve decisive victory yet neither was willing to concede, provides a sober precedent for what an unresolved conflict on Europe's eastern border may come to resemble over the years ahead<sup>[c](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Korean_War)</sup>.

### Pope Leo Issues Landmark Encyclical on AI — and Apologises for the Church's Role in Slavery

<!-- audio:top-story-3 -->
Pope Leo XIV has marked his young pontificate with two historic acts of moral authority in the space of days. His inaugural encyclical, *Magnifica Humanitas*, published on 25 May, demands that artificial intelligence be "disarmed" — subjected to robust international regulation — warning that autonomous weapons systems have already begun to move beyond meaningful human control<sup>[1](https://www.bbc.com/news/articles/cedppn6002jo)</sup>. The document calls for a new global governance framework to prevent what the Pope describes as "digital slaveries," forms of algorithmic exploitation that deepen inequality and erode human dignity<sup>[2](https://www.france24.com/en/pope-leo-warns-ai-must-be-disarmed-in-landmark-encyclical)</sup>. In a separate but related gesture, Leo also offered an unprecedented papal apology for the Vatican's historical role in legitimising the transatlantic slave trade — an acknowledgement that the Church's own teaching once provided moral cover for one of history's gravest atrocities<sup>[3](https://news.google.com/rss/search?q=site:apnews.com&hl=en-US&gl=US&ceid=US:en)</sup>.

The encyclical's sweeping scope — ranging from AI-enabled warfare to the concentration of algorithmic power in the hands of a few corporations — represents the most ambitious intervention by a pope in a global technology debate since the nuclear age. Where *Magnifica Humanitas* addresses tomorrow's dangers, the slavery apology confronts yesterday's sins, and together they signal a papacy prepared to use the Church's extraordinary moral reach to challenge both historical amnesia and contemporary power<sup>[4](https://www.npr.org/2026/05/25/nx-s1-5828375/pope-leo-to-weigh-in-on-the-perils-and-promises-of-artificial-intelligence)</sup>. The encyclical has been welcomed by European policymakers struggling to advance the AI Act's implementation and by humanitarian organisations frustrated by the pace of international accountability for autonomous weapons.

> **Historical Parallel: Pacem in Terris — The Church Confronts Nuclear Technology (1963)**
> Pope John XXIII's *Pacem in Terris*, issued in April 1963, stands as the most direct precursor to *Magnifica Humanitas*. Written just months after the Cuban Missile Crisis brought the world to the brink of nuclear annihilation, it was the first encyclical ever addressed not merely to Catholics but to "all men of good will" — a deliberate signal that the moral stakes of nuclear technology transcended denominational boundaries<sup>[a](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pacem_in_terris)</sup>. John XXIII argued that no nation had the right to settle disputes through recourse to weapons of mass destruction, and that the international community required new governance institutions — specifically endorsing the United Nations — to manage technologies that individual states could no longer control responsibly. The encyclical landed with extraordinary force: *The Washington Post* called it "the voice of the conscience of the world," and a UN conference attended by over two thousand scholars and statespeople convened two years later to discuss its implications. Leo XIV's *Magnifica Humanitas* inherits that tradition directly: just as Pacem in Terris identified the bomb as a technology requiring international constraint before it destroyed civilisation, so the new encyclical frames AI — particularly its military applications — as a force that has outrun the moral and legal frameworks designed to govern it. The parallel is not merely rhetorical; both documents arrive at moments of acute technological anxiety and both call for the same remedy — binding multilateral governance over tools of mass harm.

## World

The Ebola outbreak in Uganda continued to accelerate on 25 May, with the World Health Organisation's director-general warning that the epidemic is outpacing the international response<sup>[1](https://news.google.com/rss/search?q=site:reuters.com&hl=en-US&gl=US&ceid=US:en)</sup>. Uganda has confirmed at least seven cases and the fast-moving nature of the virus — with mortality rates often exceeding fifty per cent — has prompted urgent calls for a coordinated international mobilisation of medical personnel and supplies. The outbreak's reach has already extended beyond Uganda's borders: Italy reported two suspected Ebola cases in Lombardy on 25 May after travellers returned from the country, while Spanish health authorities confirmed a second hantavirus case in Madrid, with a dozen individuals under medical observation following cruise ship exposure<sup>[2](http://www.euronews.com/my-europe/2026/05/25/ebola-alert-in-italy-two-suspected-cases-in-lombardy-after-return-from-uganda)</sup>. European public health agencies stressed that broader infection risk remains minimal, but the convergence of two viral alerts in the same week underlines the fragility of the global disease surveillance architecture.

In South Asia, a suicide bombing near a railway track in southwest Pakistan killed at least twenty-three people on 25 May, targeting civilian infrastructure in a region long plagued by militant insurgency<sup>[3](https://news.google.com/rss/search?q=site:apnews.com&hl=en-US&gl=US&ceid=US:en)</sup>. Pakistani authorities launched an investigation to identify the perpetrators and their affiliation, with the attack renewing debate about the security of civilian transportation networks across the country's southwestern provinces. The bombing drew international condemnation and fresh calls for enhanced protective measures in conflict-affected areas.

Political unrest in South America added to a week of global instability as protesters in Bolivia's capital La Paz marched toward the presidential palace on 25–26 May, with police maintaining cordons around government buildings<sup>[4](https://www.aljazeera.com/video/newsfeed/2026/5/26/aje-onl-nf_report-bolivia-protesters-presidential-palace-250526)</sup>. The demonstrations reflect widespread public dissatisfaction with the government, though the precise grievances vary across protest groups. Bolivia has a recent history of dramatic civil unrest capable of toppling administrations, and the current mobilisation, while still fluid, represents a significant test of institutional stability in the Andean nation.

## Europe

<!-- audio:europe -->
The geopolitical future of Ukraine within European institutions took a significant step forward this week as Germany's Chancellor Merz formally proposed granting Ukraine "associate member" status in the European Union — a novel halfway house that would confer representation and close economic alignment without full voting rights<sup>[1](https://www.france24.com/en/europe/20260521-germany-s-merz-proposes-ukraine-as-eu-associate-member-without-voting-rights)</sup>. The proposal reflects the growing consensus among EU governments that the formal accession process, which typically takes over a decade, cannot adequately serve Ukraine's immediate needs while the country is actively at war. Analysts note that the idea echoes earlier phases of European integration in which the continent's institutions were adapted to serve geopolitical rather than purely procedural ends — the Marshall Plan's institutional offshoots and the gradual expansion of Community competence throughout the 1980s and 1990s providing the closest precedents<sup>[2](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Marshall_Plan)</sup>.

Across the continent, an unprecedented May heatwave sharpened the urgency of the climate debate. England was on course to break its May temperature record as the mercury approached 35°C, while Hungarian scientists warned that Budapest could experience 50°C temperatures within the coming decades if warming trends continue unchecked<sup>[3](https://www.bbc.com/news/articles/c1l22r59mn5o)</sup>. France's Atlantic coast was struck by a wave of dangerous riptides connected to the extreme heat, killing two swimmers and prompting over thirty rescue operations<sup>[4](http://www.euronews.com/2026/05/25/what-is-a-heat-dome-why-europe-is-sweltering-under-record-breaking-may-temperatures)</sup>. Spain responded with the announcement of a €9 billion climate and energy transition plan, reflecting the political pressure that extreme weather events now exert on national governments across the bloc<sup>[5](http://www.euronews.com/2026/05/26/we-can-halt-warming-and-we-must-ipcc-scientist-on-why-europe-keeps-choosing-fossil-fuels)</sup>.

In the Balkans, Belgrade was shaken by some of the most violent anti-government protests in recent memory as tens of thousands of demonstrators demanding elections clashed with riot police on 24–25 May<sup>[6](https://www.france24.com/en/protesters-clash-with-police-after-an-anti-government-rally-in-serbia-s-capital)</sup>. The upheaval reflects mounting frustration with President Vučić's government and broader questions about the trajectory of democratic governance across the Western Balkans. Serbia's aspirations for EU membership have long been complicated by its geopolitical ambiguities, including its resistance to aligning with Western sanctions on Russia<sup>[7](http://www.euronews.com/video/2026/05/25/violent-clashes-break-out-after-mass-protest-in-belgrade)</sup>.

On the cybersecurity front, Lithuania issued a stark warning that a mass data breach affecting its citizens bore the hallmarks of a state-sponsored operation by a foreign government<sup>[8](http://www.euronews.com/2026/05/25/lithuania-warns-mass-data-leak-was-work-of-foreign-country)</sup>. The attribution — made carefully but publicly — adds to a pattern of aggressive cyber operations targeting Baltic and Eastern European states, and will heighten pressure on the EU to accelerate its collective cyber-defence frameworks.

## Ireland

<!-- audio:ireland -->
Two significant matters came before the Irish Cabinet on 26 May, reflecting the breadth of foreign and domestic pressures facing the government during Ireland's EU presidency. Minister for Foreign Affairs Helen McEntee brought legislation prohibiting the import of goods from Israeli settlements in the occupied Palestinian territories — a move that would make Ireland one of the few EU states to take concrete legislative action on the occupation and one that aligns with longstanding Irish support for Palestinian self-determination<sup>[1](https://www.rte.ie/news/politics/2026/0526/1575228-cabinet-israel-trade/)</sup>. Separately, Minister for Justice Jim O'Callaghan sought approval to modify the accommodation arrangements in place for Ukrainian residents, balancing Ireland's continuing humanitarian obligations toward those displaced by Russia's invasion against the strain the country's housing system has been under since 2022<sup>[2](https://www.rte.ie/news/politics/2026/0526/1575237-cabinet-politics/)</sup>.

The trial of former DUP leader Jeffrey Donaldson opened on 26 May, marking one of the most consequential moments in recent Northern Irish political history<sup>[3](https://www.irishtimes.com/crime-law/courts/2026/05/26/former-dup-leader-jeffrey-donaldsons-sexual-offences-trial-due-to-get-under-way/)</sup>. Donaldson, who led the Democratic Unionist Party from 2021 and played a central role in the North's relationship with the post-Brexit constitutional settlement, now faces serious criminal charges. His wife Eleanor's trial of the facts runs concurrently. The case has profound implications for unionist politics at a moment when the DUP is already navigating fractious internal debates about its direction and relevance.

In a different chapter of the North's unfinished history, a search operation commenced in County Armagh on 26 May to locate the remains of Seamus Maguire, one of the Disappeared — the victims of the Troubles who were abducted, killed and secretly buried by paramilitaries<sup>[4](https://www.rte.ie/news/ireland/2026/0526/1575194-seamus-maguire-search/)</sup>. The Commission for the Location of Victims' Remains has been conducting such searches for years, and each one represents an act of belated justice for families who have waited decades for the most basic closure.

Ireland's Defence Forces on 26 May outlined the elaborate security architecture put in place to guard against drone threats during the EU presidency, including the deployment of shotguns, helicopters and specialised airburst ammunition<sup>[5](https://www.irishtimes.com/crime-law/2026/05/26/shotguns-helicopters-and-airburst-ammunition-to-guard-against-rogue-drones-during-eu-presidency/)</sup>. The measures draw on lessons from the conflict in Ukraine and reflect how the proliferation of commercial and military drone technology has fundamentally altered the security calculus even for a traditionally neutral small state hosting a major diplomatic season. The Competition and Consumer Protection Commission, meanwhile, disclosed that its blitz of eighty unannounced business inspections across six counties had uncovered numerous potential breaches of consumer law, with enforcement actions now under consideration<sup>[6](https://www.rte.ie/news/ireland/2026/0526/1575241-ccpc-inspections/)</sup>. On the political front, internal Sinn Féin voices acknowledged that two successive byelection defeats may require the party to revisit its messaging strategy, even as the leadership maintained a public posture of confidence<sup>[7](https://www.thejournal.ie/sinn-fein-byelections-7051096-May2026/)</sup>.
