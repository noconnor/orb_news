### Iran Attacks UAE Oil Infrastructure as US Military Escorts Vessels Through Strait of Hormuz

<!-- audio:top-story-1 -->
Iran launched military strikes on UAE oil facilities on 4 May, prompting an immediate US military response to escort commercial vessels through the Strait of Hormuz — the narrow channel through which roughly a fifth of global oil supply passes daily.<sup>[1](https://www.aljazeera.com/news/2026/5/5/dangerous-escalation-world-condemns-iran-after-attacks-on-uae?traffic_source=rss)</sup> The Trump administration deployed naval assets to keep the shipping lanes open as oil markets reacted sharply, with Brent crude rising close to six percent on fears of prolonged supply disruption.<sup>[2](https://www.aljazeera.com/economy/2026/5/5/oil-prices-surge-as-violence-flares-in-strait-of-hormuz?traffic_source=rss)</sup> International condemnation followed swiftly, with multiple governments describing the strikes as a dangerous escalation, while South Korea announced it was investigating a separate ship fire in the strait.<sup>[3](https://www.bbc.com/news/articles/cjwp432d0v5o?at_medium=RSS&at_campaign=rss)</sup>

The economic ripple effects extended well beyond energy markets. Germany — already reeling from an Iran-war-driven oil price surge of nearly 73 percent year-to-date — halved its 2026 GDP growth forecast to 0.5 percent, citing the continuing fallout on energy-intensive industries that employ close to a million workers.<sup>[4](https://www.euronews.com/business/2026/04/22/germany-halves-gdp-forecast-from-1-to-05-due-to-iran-war-fallout)</sup><sup>[5](https://www.cnbc.com/2026/04/24/germany-iran-defense-war-energy-oil-price-shock-industry-fiscal-stimulus-middle-east-europe-inflation-growth.html)</sup> The strikes have pushed ceasefire diplomacy to the brink, as shipping insurers raised premiums on Gulf transits and energy analysts warned that any sustained closure of the strait could trigger a recession across import-dependent economies in Asia and Europe.<sup>[6](https://www.npr.org/2026/05/04/nx-s1-5810508/iran-war-updates)</sup>

> **Historical Parallel: The Tanker War and the Iran–Iraq War (1980–1988)**
> The current crisis echoes the Tanker War of 1984–1988, when Iran and Iraq attacked hundreds of oil tankers transiting the Persian Gulf during their eight-year conflict, eventually drawing in US naval escorts under Operation Earnest Will.<sup>[7](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tanker_war)</sup> That conflict demonstrated how a regional war between two states could rapidly internationalise as great powers intervened to protect commercial shipping and energy supply lines — the same dynamic now unfolding in the Strait of Hormuz.<sup>[8](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Iran%E2%80%93Iraq_War)</sup> Operation Eagle Claw (1980) and the broader Iran–Iraq war period also established the pattern of Iranian willingness to use its geographic position astride the strait as strategic leverage, a posture that has never fully receded.<sup>[9](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Operation_Eagle_Claw)</sup> The 1973 Arab Oil Embargo and 1990 Gulf War oil price shock provide further context: each time conflict has threatened Persian Gulf supply, global markets have spiked, governments have reached for strategic reserves, and diplomatic pressure has intensified on all parties to de-escalate before recessionary damage becomes irreversible.<sup>[10](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/1973_oil_crisis)</sup><sup>[11](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/1990_oil_price_shock)</sup> The current episode appears structurally similar — a direct Iranian attack on Gulf infrastructure forcing an American military response — but with Germany's growth already suppressed and global growth still fragile, the economic tolerance for a prolonged confrontation is considerably lower than in previous cycles.

### Two Killed in Car-Ramming Attack in Leipzig as Germany Again Confronts Vehicle Violence

<!-- audio:top-story-2 -->
A 33-year-old German citizen drove a vehicle into shoppers on Grimmaische Strasse in central Leipzig on 4 May, killing two people and injuring at least 20 others in what authorities described as a suspected rampage attack.<sup>[1](https://www.bbc.com/news/articles/ckgpzgxgz58o)</sup> Police detained the driver at the scene and described him as "psychologically conspicuous," with investigators working to establish whether the attack was politically motivated or rooted in personal circumstances.<sup>[2](https://www.euronews.com/my-europe/2026/05/04/at-least-two-killed-after-car-drives-into-people-on-street-in-german-city-of-leipzig)</sup> The pedestrianised shopping street leads directly into Leipzig's city centre, and the timing — on a busy Sunday afternoon — maximised the density of potential victims.

The Leipzig attack is the latest in a series of vehicle-based incidents that have shaken public confidence in the safety of Germany's open urban spaces.<sup>[3](https://www.france24.com/en/live-news/20260504-two-dead-after-car-ploughs-into-people-in-germany-s-leipzig-mayor)</sup> The December 2024 Magdeburg Christmas market attack, in which a driver killed at least six people, prompted a national debate about security measures at public events; Leipzig now reopens that conversation before any lasting policy consensus has been reached. Across Europe, security services and urban planners have grappled with how to harden public spaces without transforming city centres into fortified enclosures — a tension that vehicle-ramming attacks, requiring minimal preparation and no specialist weaponry, make uniquely difficult to resolve.<sup>[4](https://news.google.com/rss/articles/CBMimgFBVV95cUxQdTNDYjNLRFZNNWtEaS1WdHZUZGdwcFhvb3EwVU5DNEJBXy1RMzJJc0FKSlpXT281ZzhnSUpYVERZemctc1dwYmhubDc0WTY1SU81YXA0NFRlOXQ1ZG15XzYwU3FNcmtOUV8wNGNhNENCOWw2OWg2bUFva2RmUWgyWmw0dk1QNUdGdE41dm5OdnpyVFFiNDRFRmlR?oc=5)</sup>

> **Historical Parallel: 2016 Berlin Christmas Market Attack**
> The Leipzig attack belongs to a wave of vehicle-ramming incidents in Europe that intensified after 2016, when Anis Amri drove a hijacked truck into the Christmas market at Breitscheidplatz in Berlin, killing 12 people and injuring 56 in the deadliest Islamist terrorist attack in modern German history.<sup>[Wikipedia: 2016 Berlin Christmas market attack](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/2016_Berlin_Christmas_market_attack)</sup> That attack exposed systemic failures — Amri was a known radicalisation risk who had been monitored by intelligence services yet remained free — and prompted a broad reform of German security protocols, including enhanced surveillance authority and tighter deportation procedures. The tactical simplicity of vehicle attacks, requiring no specialist skills or materials, had by then been recognised as a defining vulnerability of open urban societies, a reality underlined by the Nice truck attack of July 2016, the Westminster Bridge attack of 2017, and the Barcelona Las Ramblas attack the same year. Each incident produced fresh reviews of vehicle exclusion barriers, police response times, and intelligence-sharing between federal and state agencies. The Leipzig attack demonstrates that even after years of lessons learned and physical hardening of Christmas markets and major events, determined perpetrators can still exploit the ordinary geography of a city — a pedestrian shopping street on a Sunday afternoon — with lethal results. The recurring pattern points to a structural dilemma: tighter security at known symbolic targets displaces risk toward unprotected everyday spaces, making the problem effectively impossible to eliminate through physical means alone.

### European Leaders Gather in Armenia for EPC Summit as Zelensky Joins First Visit in 24 Years

<!-- audio:top-story-3 -->
The eighth European Political Community summit convened in Yerevan on 4–5 May, bringing together leaders from 47 European states in the first EPC gathering to be hosted by a South Caucasus nation.<sup>[1](https://kyivindependent.com/)</sup> Ukrainian President Volodymyr Zelensky attended in person — his first presidential visit to Armenia in 24 years — meeting with UK Prime Minister Keir Starmer and EU foreign policy chief Ursula von der Leyen on the margins of the summit.<sup>[2](https://www.aljazeera.com/news/2026/4/23/eu-formally-approves-90bn-euro-ukraine-loan-and-new-sanctions-on-russia)</sup> Starmer and von der Leyen discussed UK participation in the EU's 90-billion-euro Ukraine loan package, with both sides describing a possible deal as a significant step forward in the post-Brexit EU-UK defence industrial relationship.

The summit also drew Taoiseach Micheál Martin, who used bilateral conversations on the sidelines to state publicly that Ireland should "consider seriously" the nuclear power option as part of its long-term energy security strategy — a notable departure from decades of Irish political reticence on the issue, and one that reflects the broader European conversation about energy independence accelerated by the Iran conflict.<sup>[3](https://www.thejournal.ie/taoiseach-nuclear-power-energy-ireland-7030692-May2026/)</sup> The Yerevan gathering underscored how the EPC has evolved in just four years from an aspirational idea into the primary forum for pan-European security coordination, operating in parallel to — and sometimes ahead of — the slower institutional machinery of the EU itself.

> **Historical Parallel: The European Political Community (founded 2022)**
> The current European Political Community was born directly from the shock of Russia's full-scale invasion of Ukraine. French President Emmanuel Macron proposed the forum on 9 May 2022 at the Conference on the Future of Europe, and the first summit took place in Prague just five months later in October 2022, with 44 states represented — a remarkable speed of institutional creation by European standards.<sup>[Wikipedia: European Political Community](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/European_Political_Community)</sup> The EPC's design — an intergovernmental forum modelled loosely on the G7, without its own secretariat, budget, or staff — was deliberately light-touch, allowing non-EU states including the UK, Ukraine, Turkey, and the South Caucasus republics to participate on equal terms with EU members. Russia and Belarus were explicitly excluded. In its first four years the EPC has hosted summits in Moldova, Spain, the United Kingdom, Hungary, Albania, and Denmark, progressively expanding its geographic and thematic scope. The Armenia summit is the first to be held in the South Caucasus, a region where the EPC has already deployed civilian monitoring missions, and Canada's participation marks the forum's first involvement of a non-European nation. The EPC's trajectory illustrates how existential external threats can accelerate European institutional innovation faster than decades of incremental treaty reform — a dynamic with its own historical precedents in the post-war construction of NATO and the Council of Europe after 1945.
> _[Wikipedia: European Political Community](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/European_Political_Community)_

Austria took a striking diplomatic step on 4 May by expelling three Russian Embassy staff members over suspected signals intelligence operations targeting the constellation of international organisations headquartered in Vienna.<sup>[1](https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-05-04/array-of-spy-antennas-prompts-austria-to-expel-russian-envoys)</sup> Austrian authorities linked the expulsions to satellite dish installations on the roof of the Russian diplomatic compound, which they believe were used to intercept data from the International Atomic Energy Agency, OPEC, and the OSCE — institutions whose intelligence value to Moscow, particularly during the Iran conflict, is self-evident. Russia had refused to waive diplomatic immunity when Austrian prosecutors sought to question the suspects, and the Russian Embassy subsequently condemned the expulsions as "outrageous" and "politically motivated," threatening a retaliatory response. The move brings the total number of Russian diplomats expelled by Austria since 2020 to 14, a cumulative figure that underlines how the diplomatic relationship between Vienna — long a neutral city prized for its role as a meeting place — and Moscow has deteriorated since the full-scale invasion of Ukraine.<sup>[2](https://abcnews.com/International/wireStory/austria-expels-3-russian-embassy-staff-suspected-antenna-132629706)</sup><sup>[3](https://www.scmp.com/news/world/europe/article/3352390/austria-expels-3-russian-diplomats-over-signals-spying)</sup>
