### Israel Expands Ground Invasion into Lebanon as US-Iran Peace Deal Stalls

<!-- audio:top-story-1 -->
Israeli military forces have pushed beyond the Litani River into southern Lebanon for the first time since 2006, reaching the city of Nabatieh while continuing airstrikes on residential areas across the country <sup>[1](https://www.aljazeera.com/news/liveblog/2026/5/31/iran-war-live-lebanon-accuses-israel-of-scorched-earth-policy)</sup>. Lebanon's Prime Minister Nawaf Salam accused Israel of pursuing a deliberate "scorched-earth policy" and imposing collective punishment on civilian populations, raising alarm among international humanitarian organisations <sup>[1](https://www.aljazeera.com/news/liveblog/2026/5/31/iran-war-live-lebanon-accuses-israel-of-scorched-earth-policy)</sup>. The expansion marks a significant escalation of a conflict that began with joint US-Israeli operations in February targeting Iran's leadership, and has since evolved into a grinding ground war in Lebanon with mounting humanitarian consequences <sup>[2](https://www.npr.org/2026/05/29/g-s1-124776/up-first-newsletter-israel-lebanon-iran-war-e-jean-carroll-trump-cbs-60-minutes-voting)</sup>.

Diplomacy has so far failed to halt the fighting. Iran's Foreign Ministry stated that a memorandum of understanding with the United States has not yet been finalised, leaving ceasefire negotiations in an unresolved and fragile state <sup>[1](https://www.aljazeera.com/news/liveblog/2026/5/31/iran-war-live-lebanon-accuses-israel-of-scorched-earth-policy)</sup>. US Defense Secretary Pete Hegseth warned that American military forces are prepared to resume combat operations in the Gulf if necessary, and cautioned Iran against launching any strikes should peace talks ultimately collapse <sup>[2](https://www.npr.org/2026/05/29/g-s1-124776/up-first-newsletter-israel-lebanon-iran-war-e-jean-carroll-trump-cbs-60-minutes-voting)</sup>. The dual-track pressure — continued military action alongside unresolved diplomacy — has created a volatile environment in which any miscalculation risks broadening an already dangerous regional conflict.

> **Historical Parallel: The 1982 Lebanon War and the 2006 Lebanon War (1982; 2006)**
> Israel's advance beyond the Litani River echoes two prior incursions into Lebanese territory that each began with defined military objectives and ended in prolonged entanglement. In 1982, Israel launched Operation Peace for Galilee, driving deep into Lebanon and reaching Beirut under the stated goal of eliminating PLO infrastructure — a campaign that instead produced an eighteen-year occupation and the conditions from which Hezbollah emerged <sup>_[Wikipedia: 1982 Lebanon War](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/1982_Lebanon_War)_</sup>. The 2006 war, triggered by Hezbollah's cross-border raid, ended with UN Resolution 1701 establishing a ceasefire and an expanded UNIFIL mandate — but left the underlying political dynamics largely unresolved, with Hezbollah emerging politically strengthened despite military losses <sup>_[Wikipedia: 2006 Lebanon War](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/2006_Lebanon_War)_</sup>. The Tanker War of 1984–1988 and the Iran-Iraq War offer further context for the regional dimension: they demonstrated how conflicts centred on Iran tend to draw in external powers, disrupt Gulf energy flows, and resist clean diplomatic resolution, with the US eventually drawn into direct naval confrontations despite initial efforts to remain on the margins <sup>_[Wikipedia: Tanker War](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tanker_war)_</sup> <sup>_[Wikipedia: Iran-Iraq War](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Iran%E2%80%93Iraq_War)_</sup>. The current stalled US-Iran negotiations carry echoes of these patterns: the combination of ongoing military operations, incomplete ceasefire frameworks, and a US executive branch asserting expansive military authority without clear congressional authorisation recalls the conditions that eventually prompted the War Powers Resolution — and the persistent difficulty of using it as a constraint <sup>_[Wikipedia: War Powers Resolution](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/War_Powers_Resolution)_</sup>. What the historical record consistently shows is that Israeli ground operations in Lebanon tend to achieve initial tactical gains while generating strategic complications that outlast the original military rationale.

### China's Rapid Military Buildup Reshapes Pacific Security as Japan and Taiwan Respond

<!-- audio:top-story-2 -->
Japan's defence establishment has moved to rebuff accusations of resurgent militarism, redirecting attention to what it describes as China's accelerating military expansion as the primary driver of instability across the Indo-Pacific region <sup>[1](https://www.npr.org/2026/05/29/g-s1-124788/asia-defense-summit-opens-amid-doubts-over-u-s-priorities)</sup>. Taiwan scrambled ships and fighter jets this week to shadow the second Chinese "joint combat readiness patrol" in as many days, underscoring Beijing's sustained pressure campaign around the island and the frequency with which Chinese forces are now testing Taiwan's response capabilities <sup>[1](https://www.npr.org/2026/05/29/g-s1-124788/asia-defense-summit-opens-amid-doubts-over-u-s-priorities)</sup>. Analysts at the Shangri-La regional security dialogue in Singapore put numbers to the scale of the challenge: China's People's Liberation Army is estimated to be producing approximately 120 J-20A fifth-generation stealth fighters annually, a rate that exceeds current US Air Force F-35 procurement and is rapidly eroding Western assumptions about air superiority in the Pacific <sup>[2](https://news.google.com/rss/articles/CBMiogFBVV95cUxPdmlJTHUyX0Z5aVh6RXE3aThmbDRINlhvcTNoMElRRHoxaXpnT1JNR0NUWnppNE5DSEdsaGFGQVM3ZWtmV0ZmWHVuV09RRTlPemFqcEJNWWEwSURpSW80WVdTT1pjOEFhVHEtQ3JyTWx2MGQwX0JxME5sTjUweXZKRTY1SFh5ZjlnX3NkVXpka3VHZjd3UXlzRzFIbDdOZGR6YVE)</sup>.

Beyond the Taiwan Strait, China is systematically expanding its military footprint across the Southwest Pacific, the Indian Ocean, and Australia's maritime approaches — normalising its defence presence through incremental, long-term positioning rather than dramatic provocations <sup>[2](https://news.google.com/rss/articles/CBMiogFBVV95cUxPdmlJTHUyX0Z5aVh6RXE3aThmbDRINlhvcTNoMElRRHoxaXpnT1JNR0NUWnppNE5DSEdsaGFGQVM3ZWtmV0ZmWHVuV09RRTlPemFqcEJNWWEwSURpSW80WVdTT1pjOEFhVHEtQ3JyTWx2MGQwX0JxME5sTjUweXZKRTY1SFh5ZjlnX3NkVXpka3VHZjd3UXlzRzFIbDdOZGR6YVE)</sup>. US Defense Secretary Pete Hegseth, speaking at the summit, acknowledged "rightful alarm" over the pace of the Chinese military buildup while adopting a more measured tone on the broader state of US-China relations — a careful calibration that reflects Washington's difficulty in sustaining both competitive deterrence and functional economic and diplomatic engagement with Beijing <sup>[1](https://www.npr.org/2026/05/29/g-s1-124788/asia-defense-summit-opens-amid-doubts-over-u-s-priorities)</sup>. Japan's own accelerating defence spending, meanwhile, represents the sharpest break from its post-war strategic posture in decades, driven by a government that has concluded that Article 9 pacifism alone can no longer guarantee national security in the current regional environment.

> **Historical Parallel: The Soviet Naval Buildup Under Admiral Gorshkov (1956–1985)**
> The transformation of the Soviet Navy from a coastal defence force into a global strategic rival to the United States Navy under Fleet Admiral Sergei Gorshkov offers the most instructive modern precedent for what China is now pursuing across the Pacific. When Gorshkov took command in 1956, the Soviet Navy was a limited, second-tier force; by 1990 it had grown to over 1,000 ships and 1,170 aircraft, with submarines, missile-armed surface vessels, and naval aviation units deployed across the Mediterranean, the Indian Ocean, and the Pacific — waters the US Navy had previously treated as its operational preserve <sup>_[Wikipedia: Soviet Navy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Soviet_Navy)_</sup>. Gorshkov's strategy deliberately avoided matching Western carrier power where it was strongest, instead building asymmetric capabilities — fast missile boats, anti-carrier submarines, land-based maritime strike aircraft — designed to deny the US Navy freedom of movement and disrupt Western sea lines of communication. By the 1970s, Soviet submarine technology in some categories exceeded that of the United States, forcing a comprehensive and expensive US Navy response. China's current approach — outpacing F-35 production with the J-20A, expanding naval reach into distant waters, and applying sustained pressure across multiple theatres simultaneously — follows a recognisably Gorshkovian logic: not to win a direct confrontation, but to erode the margins of Western superiority until the strategic balance shifts. The principal difference is that China is doing this from a far larger economic base and with faster industrial capacity than the Soviet Union ever achieved, making the pace of the challenge considerably more acute than anything Gorshkov's planners could have managed.

### Norway Becomes Ninth State Under France's Nuclear Deterrence Framework

<!-- audio:top-story-3 -->
Norway formally joined France's nuclear deterrence initiative on 27 May, with President Emmanuel Macron and Norwegian Prime Minister Jonas Gahr Støre signing the agreement in Paris, making Norway the ninth European country to come under France's "forward nuclear deterrence" doctrine <sup>[1](https://www.france24.com/en/europe/20250527-norway-becomes-ninth-country-to-come-under-french-nuclear-umbrella)</sup>. The previous eight signatories — Belgium, Denmark, Germany, Greece, the Netherlands, Poland, Sweden, and the United Kingdom — represent the core of Europe's Atlantic-facing security architecture, and Norway's addition signals that even countries with long-standing, non-nuclear traditions within NATO now regard the French umbrella as a meaningful supplement to American guarantees <sup>[2](https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-05-27/macron-says-norway-agrees-to-work-closely-on-nuclear-deterrence)</sup>. Oslo was explicit that the agreement does not imply the deployment of French nuclear weapons on Norwegian territory in peacetime, framing the arrangement as an additional security layer within NATO rather than a departure from alliance commitments or a rejection of US extended deterrence <sup>[1](https://www.france24.com/en/europe/20250527-norway-becomes-ninth-country-to-come-under-french-nuclear-umbrella)</sup>.

The framework's expansion reflects a broader European reckoning with strategic autonomy that has accelerated sharply since 2022. For Macron, the initiative fulfils a longstanding ambition to position France's independent nuclear deterrent — the force de frappe — not merely as a national asset but as a European strategic resource, giving Paris both influence and responsibility at the centre of the continent's security debate <sup>[2](https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-05-27/macron-says-norway-agrees-to-work-closely-on-nuclear-deterrence)</sup>. The growing list of signatories also indicates that European governments increasingly view France's willingness to extend deterrence as credible — a political judgment that would have seemed improbable a decade ago, when doubts about whether any French president would genuinely risk Paris for Warsaw or Oslo were routine.

> **Historical Parallel: European Security Architecture and US Commitment — Exercise REFORGER and France's Withdrawal from NATO's Integrated Command (1966–1993)**
> The current expansion of France's nuclear umbrella across nine European allies draws on two distinct historical currents in European security. The first is the legacy of France's 1966 withdrawal from NATO's integrated military command under de Gaulle — a unilateral assertion of strategic autonomy that was widely dismissed at the time but which preserved France's independent nuclear force as a sovereign instrument, now available as the foundation for exactly the kind of European security leadership Macron is exercising <sup>_[Wikipedia: France and NATO](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/France_and_NATO)_</sup>. The second is the long arc of US commitment to European defence through REFORGER — the annual exercises from 1969 to 1993 that demonstrated America's willingness to surge forces back into Western Europe — which created a security architecture so dependent on US forward presence that any perceived weakening of that commitment, as has been evident in recent years, triggers precisely the kind of alternative European arrangements now taking shape <sup>_[Wikipedia: Exercise REFORGER](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Exercise_REFORGER)_</sup>. The post-Cold War drawdown of US Army Europe forces, from roughly 200,000 personnel at peak to a fraction of that today, removed much of the physical underpinning of the old guarantee, leaving European governments to conclude that sovereignty over their own deterrence is no longer a luxury but a strategic necessity <sup>_[Wikipedia: United States Army Europe and Africa](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/United_States_Army_Europe_and_Africa)_</sup>. Norway's accession to the French framework is, in this sense, less a departure from the transatlantic order than a pragmatic adaptation to its changing terms.

The most dramatic single event of the weekend came not from any conflict zone but from Budapest's Puskás Aréna, where Paris Saint-Germain defeated Arsenal 4–3 on penalties to retain the UEFA Champions League title on 30 May <sup>[1](https://www.bbc.com/news/articles/cg7pk5j7nyjo?at_medium=RSS&at_campaign=rss)</sup>. The celebrations that followed in Paris quickly turned chaotic, with thousands of supporters converging on public spaces and French police arresting hundreds as flares were fired and public disorder spread across multiple arrondissements — a recurring pattern whenever PSG wins major silverware that has now prompted questions about how French authorities plan crowd management for next season's inevitable repeat <sup>[1](https://www.bbc.com/news/articles/cg7pk5j7nyjo?at_medium=RSS&at_campaign=rss)</sup>.

Elsewhere in Latin America, the two most significant political contests of the southern spring unfolded simultaneously. Colombian voters cast ballots in the first round of a presidential election to choose a successor to Gustavo Petro, with left-wing senator Iván Cepeda leading a three-way field that also includes right-wing outsider Abelardo de la Espriella and centre-right candidate Paloma Valencia — all running against a backdrop of persistent violence and corruption that has defined Colombian politics for generations <sup>[3](https://www.aljazeera.com/news/2026/5/30/opposite-visions-what-to-know-about-colombias-presidential-election)</sup>. Polling suggests Cepeda is likely to top the first round without reaching the fifty-percent threshold required to avoid a runoff, setting up a competitive second vote on 21 June <sup>[3](https://www.aljazeera.com/news/2026/5/30/opposite-visions-what-to-know-about-colombias-presidential-election)</sup>. Meanwhile in Lima, thousands of Peruvians marched in protest against the presidential candidacy of Keiko Fujimori, whose father Alberto Fujimori ruled Peru from 1990 to 2000 in a period that combined economic stabilisation with serious human rights abuses — a legacy that the scale of Sunday's demonstrations suggests remains a raw political wound for a substantial portion of the electorate <sup>[4](https://www.aljazeera.com/video/newsfeed/2026/5/31/aje-onl-nf_clip-thousands-in-peru-protest-fujimori-run-300526)</sup>.

In Southeast Asia, rescue teams in Laos continued to pull survivors from a flooded cave system that has held the country's attention through the weekend, with four additional villagers extracted following the first successful rescue on Friday <sup>[5](https://www.aljazeera.com/video/newsfeed/2026/5/31/aje-onl-nf_more-survivors-pulled-from-flooded-laos-cave-300526)</sup>. The operation has required days of dangerous diving in confined, water-filled passages and has drawn both local and international rescue expertise to a region where monsoon season creates this kind of emergency with some regularity. Across the border in eastern Syria, rescue workers pulled stranded farmers from floodwaters after the Euphrates River burst its banks, the swollen river sweeping through agricultural land and isolating rural communities already burdened by years of compound humanitarian stress <sup>[6](https://www.aljazeera.com/video/newsfeed/2026/5/31/rescues-in-eastern-syria-after-the-euphrates-river-bursts-its-banks)</sup>. The two flood events, separated by hundreds of kilometres, reflect a regional pattern of seasonal hydrological crises that stretch emergency response capacity across governments with limited resources.

## Europe

<!-- audio:europe -->
Ukraine's military conducted overnight operations on 30 May targeting Russian oil infrastructure in occupied territories while continuing to press forward on the southern front, with forces reporting the liberation of the village of Novoselivka in Dnipropetrovsk Oblast <sup>[1](https://kyivindependent.com/)</sup>. The dual-track campaign — conventional territorial advances combined with deep strikes on energy and logistics infrastructure — reflects Ukraine's attempt to sustain pressure on Russian supply lines and revenue streams as the war enters what increasingly appears to be a grinding, attritional phase <sup>[2](https://www.euronews.com/video/2026/05/30/latest-news-bulletin-may-30th-2026-morning)</sup>. International support, while continuing, has diversified into increasingly indirect forms: Japan's contribution through NATO weapons acquisition channels, totalling over fourteen million dollars in military equipment, underscores how the conflict has been reframed as a matter of global rules-based order rather than a narrowly European security concern, even as Japan restricts itself to non-lethal assistance in line with its constitutional constraints <sup>[1](https://kyivindependent.com/)</sup>.

Within the European Union, lawmakers concluded an agreement that significantly rewrites the enforcement timeline of the AI Act, the bloc's landmark artificial intelligence regulation adopted in June 2024 <sup>[3](https://www.consilium.europa.eu/en/press/press-releases/2026/05/07/artificial-intelligence-council-and-parliament-agree-to-simplify-and-streamline-rules/)</sup>. Under the Digital Omnibus revisions agreed on 7 May, the compliance deadline for high-risk AI systems in use-based applications was extended from August 2026 to December 2027, and for product-regulated systems from August 2027 to August 2028 — a significant concession to industry arguments that the original timeline was commercially impractical <sup>[4](https://www.euronews.com/my-europe/2026/05/21/the-eu-simplified-its-toughest-ai-law-what-changed-and-why-it-matters)</sup>. The same agreement introduced new prohibitions on AI-generated non-consensual intimate imagery and child sexual abuse material, effective from December 2026, reflecting the areas where political consensus around swift action was strongest even as broader compliance timelines were relaxed <sup>[3](https://www.consilium.europa.eu/en/press/press-releases/2026/05/07/artificial-intelligence-council-and-parliament-agree-to-simplify-and-streamline-rules/)</sup>.

In the Austrian Alps, approximately 3,000 protesters led by Mayor Karl Mühlsteiger of Gries am Brenner blocked the Brenner motorway — one of the most heavily trafficked transit corridors in Europe — for eight hours on 31 May, shutting the A13 motorway and connecting roads in both directions from 11:00 am to 7:00 pm <sup>[5](https://www.euronews.com/my-europe/2026/05/30/austrian-protesters-shut-brenner-motorway-on-saturday-over-pollution)</sup>. The action targeted what Tyrolean communities describe as an unsustainable intensification of transit traffic: lorry numbers on the route have more than doubled since 2000, and nearly 11 million cars and 2.5 million commercial vehicles now use the corridor annually, producing noise and particulate pollution that residents say has fundamentally degraded their quality of life <sup>[6](https://www.usnews.com/news/world/articles/2026-05-30/austrian-protesters-shut-vital-motorway-connecting-germany-to-italy)</sup>. Despite advance warnings of significant disruption, motorway operator reports suggested actual traffic impacts were limited, which may reflect the protest's success in making a political point without generating the kind of widespread inconvenience that turns public opinion against demonstrators.
