### US and Iran Exchange Strikes as Ceasefire Talks Teeter

<!-- audio:top-story-1 -->
The United States military conducted its second round of strikes against Iranian targets within three days on May 28, targeting four Iranian drones and a ground control station near the Strait of Hormuz and in Bandar Abbas <sup>[1](https://www.bbc.com/news/articles/c98r2qy5809o)</sup>. According to US Central Command, the systems destroyed posed an imminent threat to regional security and were on the verge of launching further attacks. Iran's Islamic Revolutionary Guard Corps retaliated by striking a US airbase near Bandar Abbas, marking a sharp escalation in hostilities that have been simmering since a three-month regional conflict earlier this year <sup>[2](http://www.euronews.com/2026/05/28/us-military-conducts-second-strike-against-iran-within-days-as-trump-fumes-over-negotiatio)</sup>. The exchange of blows comes at a particularly delicate moment: ceasefire negotiations are reportedly under way, and the strikes have threatened to unravel diplomatic progress on both sides.

Global oil markets responded immediately to the military action, with prices jumping sharply as traders priced in the risk of supply disruptions through the Strait of Hormuz — the critical chokepoint through which roughly 20% of the world's oil passes <sup>[3](https://www.bbc.com/news/articles/cp3p62xddq4o)</sup>. The volatility has broader economic implications for Europe and Asia, which depend heavily on Middle Eastern energy exports. Meanwhile, Israel simultaneously designated a large swathe of southern Lebanon south of the Zahrani River as a combat zone, ordering mass civilian evacuations — a development that threatens to widen the regional arc of conflict <sup>[4](https://www.bbc.com/news/articles/cj3pgrpmlklo)</sup>. The convergence of US-Iran hostilities, Israeli military posturing in Lebanon, and fragile ceasefire diplomacy underscores just how close the Middle East is to a broader conflagration.

> **Historical Parallel: The Tanker War and Iran-Iraq Conflict (1980–1988)**
> The current pattern of US strikes against Iranian assets and retaliatory Iranian actions echoes the grinding, episodic hostilities of the 1980s Gulf region. During the Iran-Iraq War — which ran from 1980 to 1988 — both nations attacked oil tankers in the Persian Gulf in what became known as the Tanker War, deliberately targeting the energy supply lines of their adversaries and neutral shipping alike <sup>[a](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tanker_war)</sup>. The United States eventually intervened directly by re-flagging Kuwaiti tankers in 1987 and engaging Iranian naval forces, coming dangerously close to a broader confrontation. Operation Eagle Claw in 1980 — the failed US military mission to rescue hostages in Tehran — illustrated how quickly American military action against Iran could spiral in unexpected directions <sup>[b](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Operation_Eagle_Claw)</sup>. The 1973 Arab Oil Embargo demonstrated what happens when Middle East conflict genuinely disrupts global energy markets: oil prices quadrupled within months, triggering recession across the Western world <sup>[c](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/1973_oil_crisis)</sup>. The present situation — with strikes continuing alongside ceasefire talks, and oil prices already spiking — is following a familiar script where military escalation and diplomatic negotiation run on parallel, often colliding tracks. The War Powers Resolution of 1973 was itself born from congressional frustration at presidents conducting sustained military operations without formal authorisation <sup>[d](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/War_Powers_Resolution)</sup> — a tension that resurfaces every time US forces engage in the Gulf region. History suggests these cycles of strike, retaliation, and negotiation can persist for years without full resolution, while the economic costs accumulate globally.

### Record May Heat Sweeps Europe, Kills and Breaks Records from Paris to Shannon

<!-- audio:top-story-2 -->
An exceptionally early and intense heat wave is shattering temperature records across Europe this May, with deaths reported as populations and public health systems struggle with the extreme conditions <sup>[1](https://www.aljazeera.com/video/newsfeed/2026/5/28/parisians-defy-swimming-ban-to-cool-off-as-record-heat-hits-europe)</sup>. Major European cities are experiencing temperatures far outside normal seasonal ranges, driving health emergencies and overwhelming emergency services. In Ireland, the thermometer at Shannon Airport reached a provisional 30.5 degrees Celsius on May 28 — smashing the previous May record of 28.4°C set in 1997 by a full two degrees — while Met Éireann extended thunderstorm warnings to seven counties as unstable conditions accompanied the heat <sup>[2](https://www.irishtimes.com/ireland/2026/05/27/met-eireann-extends-thunderstorm-warning-to-seven-counties/)</sup>. The meteorological service has explicitly connected the record-breaking event to the broader pattern of climate change, noting that extreme weather events of this kind are occurring with increasing frequency and intensity.

The combination of record-shattering warmth and severe thunderstorm warnings illustrates the volatile and unpredictable character of European weather under climate change — a situation that no longer fits into historical seasonal norms <sup>[3](https://www.rte.ie/news/2026/0528/1575598-bloom/)</sup>. The heat is being felt continent-wide, from the Atlantic seaboard to central Europe, and analysts warn that without meaningful intervention in greenhouse gas emissions, events like this will become the new normal rather than exceptional occurrences.

> **Historical Parallel: The 1976 European Heatwave**
> The current heat event recalls the devastating summer of 1976, when a massive high-pressure system settled over much of Europe from late May and refused to shift until the end of August — a meteorological blockade with no modern precedent at the time <sup>[a](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/1976_European_heatwave)</sup>. Temperatures in the United Kingdom exceeded 32°C every day for an extended period, with Cheltenham recording 35.9°C on July 3rd — figures that had been unthinkable for a British summer. England and Wales endured their longest dry spell since 1727, lasting sixteen continuous weeks, while crops failed on a massive scale and food prices rose sharply in the aftermath. The drought was so severe that the British Parliament was forced to pass emergency legislation — the Drought Act 1976 — to ration water use, and the summer became embedded in cultural memory as a defining moment of climatic extremity. Across the broader European continent, the 1976 event laid bare how vulnerable modern societies are to sustained extreme heat, from infrastructure and agriculture to public health. What was once a once-in-a-generation anomaly has, under accelerating climate change, become an increasingly regular feature of the European summer calendar — the 2003 heatwave killed tens of thousands, and now in 2026 a record-breaking May event is already claiming lives. The 1976 parallel also matters geopolitically: just as that summer forced emergency legislative and policy responses, today's extremes are intensifying pressure on European governments to accelerate climate action and harden infrastructure against heat.

Uganda moved swiftly to close its border with the DRC for four weeks as a containment measure, reflecting the acute fear among neighbouring states that the outbreak could spill across national boundaries <sup>[2](https://www.aljazeera.com/video/newsfeed/2026/5/28/uganda-closes-border-with-drc-in-an-effort-to-contain-ebola-outbreak)</sup>. ## Europe

<!-- audio:europe -->
The most significant European security development of the day is the landmark defence and security treaty signed by UK Prime Minister Keir Starmer and Polish Prime Minister Donald Tusk on May 28, described as a "generational uplift" in the bilateral relationship <sup>[1](https://www.france24.com/en/europe/20260527-starmer-and-tusk-sign-uk%E2%80%93poland-defence-pact-amid-warnings-over-russian-threats)</sup>. The agreement covers joint military capabilities, cyber defence, and coordinated response to Russian aggression, and includes plans for co-development of medium-range air defence systems and advanced missile technologies <sup>[2](https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-05-26/uk-poland-sign-defense-and-security-pact-to-curb-russia-threat)</sup>. The UK-Poland pact follows similar agreements London has reached with Germany and France in recent months, suggesting a deliberate British strategy of bilateral security integration with its closest European neighbours — a notable development given the shadow cast over UK-EU relations since Brexit. For Poland, which shares a border with both Russia and Belarus and hosts significant NATO forces, the treaty represents a further consolidation of its position as one of the most security-conscious states on the continent.

Closely related is Norway's formal accession to France's nuclear deterrence framework, becoming the ninth European nation to come under the French nuclear umbrella <sup>[3](https://www.france24.com/en/europe/20260527-norway-becomes-ninth-country-to-come-under-french-nuclear-umbrella)</sup>. Under the arrangement, France's strategic air forces can spread across member states to complicate adversary targeting calculations; Norway has stipulated it will neither station nuclear weapons on its soil in peacetime nor contribute financially to France's nuclear programme, but its participation nonetheless marks a significant shift for a traditionally Atlanticist country <sup>[4](https://easternherald.com/2026/05/28/norway-joins-france-nuclear-deterrence-scheme-macron-store/)</sup>. Together, the UK-Poland treaty and Norway's nuclear umbrella accession reflect a broader reconfiguration of European security architecture — one driven by persistent Russian military pressure and growing uncertainty about the reliability of American guarantees.

On the domestic EU policy front, the European Parliament and member states have failed to reach agreement on proposed legislation to accelerate migrant deportations, including the creation of return centres outside EU borders <sup>[5](https://www.euronews.com/my-europe/2026/05/21/eu-countries-and-parliament-clash-on-controversial-return-rules-for-migrants)</sup>. The deadlock on the "return regulation" — which would introduce longer detention, entry bans, and offshore deportation facilities — exposes the continuing depth of divisions within Europe on migration policy, even as the EU reports a 26% fall in illegal border crossings in 2025. The comprehensive Pact on Migration and Asylum is due to take full effect on June 12, marking a significant policy milestone, but the political battles over how tough deportation rules should be remain far from resolved. Separately, the European Parliament's Economic and Monetary Affairs Committee questioned Eurogroup President Kyriakos Pierrakakis on the eurozone's resilience in the face of Middle East instability and structural reform pressures <sup>[6](https://european-union.europa.eu/news-and-events_en)</sup>, while the EU presses ahead with the rollout of its ETS2 emissions trading system covering road transport and building heating — a signal that climate ambition is not being sacrificed despite elevated energy prices. Albania and Montenegro's EU accession negotiations continue to advance, with the European Parliament's Foreign Affairs Committee evaluating the progress of both Balkan states towards membership — a slow-moving but geopolitically significant process that would extend the EU's democratic perimeter further into southeastern Europe <sup>[7](https://www.europarl.europa.eu/news/en/agenda)</sup>.

## Ireland

<!-- audio:ireland -->
Ireland's domestic story today is dominated by two interwoven threads: a record-smashing heat event and the economic burden that comes with being home to one of the world's densest concentrations of data centres. While Shannon Airport's 30.5°C record has already been covered in today's top stories, the extraordinary weather has also provided a striking backdrop to the Bloom gardening festival in Dublin's Phoenix Park, which expects approximately 100,000 visitors over its run <sup>[1](https://www.rte.ie/news/2026/0528/1575598-bloom/)</sup>. This year's Bloom places a strong emphasis on biodiversity, climate action, and mental health, with Met Éireann itself contributing a dedicated climate resilience garden — a timely statement given that the meteorological service was simultaneously issuing thunderstorm warnings for seven counties <sup>[2](https://www.irishtimes.com/ireland/2026/05/27/met-eireann-puts-the-focus-on-climate-resilience-with-timely-bloom-show-garden/)</sup>. The juxtaposition captures something of Ireland's current relationship with climate: public engagement and enthusiasm on one hand, genuine meteorological disruption on the other.

New research by Friends of the Earth reveals that data centres now account for 22% of Ireland's total electricity consumption — a share that has translated into approximately €1.4 billion per year in additional costs passed on to households through higher electricity bills <sup>[3](https://www.thejournal.ie/readme/data-centres-and-climate-7052694-May2026/)</sup>. The finding sharpens the debate about the trade-off between Ireland's attractiveness as a destination for Big Tech investment and the real economic burden placed on ordinary consumers <sup>[4](https://www.irishexaminer.com/news/arid-41852406.html)</sup>. The government has faced mounting criticism over its climate action record more broadly, with new projections showing that significant emission reductions will not be achieved until the early 2030s — well behind EU-mandated targets <sup>[5](https://www.irishtimes.com/environment/2026/05/27/government-accused-of-famine-of-ambition-on-climate-action-after-latest-projection-on-missed-targets/)</sup>. Opposition parties and environmental organisations have accused the administration of a "famine of ambition," warning that the policy gap between stated goals and real-world outcomes is widening in the energy, transport, and agriculture sectors.

On the security front, twenty primary and secondary schools across County Carlow received threatening emails on May 27 warning of a planned mass shooting, prompting the Garda Síochána to deploy patrols to all primary schools in the county as a precautionary measure <sup>[6](https://www.irishexaminer.com/news/courtandcrime/arid-41852496.html)</sup>.
